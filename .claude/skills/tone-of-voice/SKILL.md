## 1. The Dataships Voice

Dataships sounds like a sharp operator who already knows your stack, your numbers, and your pain points and is handing you the playbook to fix them. We are direct, data-dense, and operator-fluent. We don't lecture. We don't hedge. We show you the math, point to the proof, and make it easy to act.

**The Voice in One Sentence:** Confident, data-driven, and operator-fluent. We talk to DTC leaders like peers, not prospects.

### 1.1 Core Voice Attributes

| Attribute | What It Means | How It Shows Up |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct** | Lead with the point. No preamble, no throat-clearing. | Short sentences. Active voice. Numbers before narrative. |
| **Data-Dense** | Every claim is backed by a metric, a case study, or a benchmark. | Specific percentages, dollar amounts, ROI multiples. Never vague. |
| **Operator-Fluent** | We speak the language of ecom operators: CLV, AOV, opt-in rate, flows. | Use industry terms without defining them. Our reader already knows. |
| **Confident (Not Arrogant)** | We state what we know with authority. We don't oversell or make empty promises. | "We get 3-5X higher sign-up rates" not "We might help improve your rates." |
| **Peer-to-Peer** | We talk alongside our buyers, not down to them. | "You know checkout is under-optimized" not "Did you know checkout could be better?" |
| **Action-Oriented** | Every piece of content should make the reader want to do something. | Clear CTAs. Free A/B test offers. Specific next steps. |

### 1.2 Vocabulary Rules

**Always say:**
- Consent optimization platform
- Consent infrastructure
- Privacy rules engine
- Marketing consent rate (MCR)
- Incremental LTV / repeat revenue
- High-intent buyers / paying customers
- A/B test (not "trial" or "pilot")
- Opt-in rate (not "subscription rate")
- Network intelligence / network effects

**Never say:**
- Widget / app / tool / checkbox optimizer
- Compliance platform (we're growth-first, compliance-built)
- "Simple change to consent language"
- "Tweak the checkout" or "optimize the checkbox"
- "We grow LTV with dynamic consent collection"
- Popup / lead capture / exit-intent
- Identity resolution / anonymous visitor tracking

### 1.3 Data Presentation Standards

Data is the backbone of our credibility. Every content piece should include at least one concrete metric.

**Formatting rules:**
- Use specific numbers, not ranges: "97% opt-in rate" not "significantly higher opt-ins"
- Always provide context: "97% opt-in rate (up from 39%)" is stronger than "97% opt-in rate"
- Pair percentages with dollar amounts when possible: "+34% opt-in rate -> $363K in repeat revenue"
- Use the before/after format for maximum impact: "39% -> 81%" or "0.36% -> 12%"
- Attribute data to real brands: "Ekster drove $363K" not "one customer drove $363K"
- ROI multiples are powerful closers: "25X ROI" or "491% ROI in 12 months"
- For compliance stats, cite the regulation count: "19 US state privacy laws and counting"

### 1.4 Do / Don't Quick Reference

| DO | DON'T |
|---|---|
| Lead with the metric or outcome | Lead with product features |
| Use active voice: "Ekster drove $363K" | Use passive voice: "$363K was generated" |
| Reference specific brands and case studies | Use vague social proof: "our customers see results" |
| Address the reader as "you" | Address the reader as "brands" or "merchants" in 2nd person |
| Frame consent as revenue infrastructure | Frame consent as a compliance checkbox |
| Offer a concrete next step (A/B test, demo) | End with a vague "learn more" |
| Write like a peer sharing intel | Write like a vendor pitching a product |
| Show the math: "X -> Y = $Z" | Make unsupported claims: "dramatically increases revenue" |
| Keep sentences short and punchy | Write long, meandering paragraphs |
| Use industry terms (CLV, AOV, MCR) | Over-explain basics your buyer already knows |

### 1.5 Hard QA Filters (Universal)

**These filters apply to ALL Dataships content, regardless of surface or format. No exceptions. Every piece must teach, prove, or entertain. Never fill space.**

Run these checks on every draft before delivering. If any pattern appears, rewrite it.

**Banned patterns:**

1. **Rhetorical Q&A setups:** "The result?" "What happened next?" "So what does this mean?" These patterns feel manufactured. State the point directly instead of staging a reveal.

2. **"No X. No Y. Just Z." constructions:** "No popups. No gimmicks. Just results." This cadence is overused in SaaS marketing and undermines our credibility. Rewrite as a direct statement.

3. **Dramatic reveal tropes:** "And then everything changed." "Not anymore." "Enter Dataships." These are cheap narrative devices. Let the data create the drama.

4. **Sentence fragments used for emphasis:** "Not the offer. Or the copy. The consent." Staccato fragments used as rhetorical flourish. Write complete sentences that carry their own weight.

5. **Overuse of em dashes:** Em dashes are useful for parenthetical asides but become a crutch when overused. Limit to 2-3 per piece. If you find more, rewrite using commas, colons, or separate sentences.

6. **Generic hype terms:** "Game-changing," "cutting-edge," "revolutionary," "next-level," "best-in-class." These are empty calories. Replace with a specific metric or proof point every time.

**The Gut Check:** Before publishing any piece, ask: Does every sentence teach the reader something, prove a claim, or earn their attention? If a sentence does none of those three things, cut it.
